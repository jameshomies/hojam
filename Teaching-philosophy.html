Teaching Philosophy



The hybrid or "flipped" classroom model has been the standard for the teaching team within the chemistry department for as long as I have been on the teaching team. Here, students learn the material at home through assigned readings and videos and during class, work with other students and the teaching team for in class problems. Since my time here as a teaching assistant (going into my third year), I have yet to have met one student out of the 2000+ students in general chemistry who've expressed satisfaction of this teaching style.

The overarching consensus appears to be the theme "I pay thousands of dollars in tuition to be taught in class, not to watch videos and be assigned time-consuming problems". My view on this argument is simple: is this really the value of what I can bring to your education? If I ever have the honor and fortune of standing in front of the podium as a professor in teaching a course such as general chemistry, having attained immense knowledge and dedication on the subject, is the value I can bring as an educator only limited to spewing out content that a simple Google search could answer, or Youtube video could explain just as well? I guarantee you, the stoichiometry I am brandishing on a white board is not any different than what you may find in Khan Academy. I have not re-invented the ideal gas law or the quantum numbers.

The value of an educator is not limited merely based off of what they know, but how their delivery of said knowledge impacts their students. I value "doing" rather than spectating, and being an active participant in one's education rather than a bystander to a traditional lecture. Knowledge is ineffectual if it cannot be applied, and so I value systematic guided approach to problem solving. Rather than give clues on how to approach a problem, I ask them what they wrote in their notes based off the last lecture and see if they can draw any parallels to the problem. I seldom respond to any direct questions, instead, I prefer to respond with my own question to stimulate critical thinking and overaching understanding of game plan.

One of my favorite anecdotes comes from being a learning assistant for chemical engineering fluids transport phenomena. During a midterm review session I had derived an equation for the pressure inside a capillary in a space station with a droplet attached at the end. The pressure balance is simple, there is only the interfacial pressure of the capillary and droplet and atmospheric pressure. On the exam, there was a question asking about the pressure of a droplet in space without the capillary. Most of the students on the exam were unable to correctly come to the right solution. Many of them had pulled up my slides on their computers during the test staring at my derivation (it was open notes) wondering how to apply it to this problem. Virtually every example in class regarding surface tension involved a capillary. Now, for those of you wondering, this should be an even simpler problem than the former as the pressure of the droplet is simply equal to the interfacial pressure of the droplet and atmospheric. I maintain that doing, rather than watching is the best method of learning. Critically thinking about the concepts and how they can be applied is more strategic sense than watching me derive equations for nuanced situations, hoping that a one-size-fits-all equation is handed.

The goal to solving a problem is to not get the right answer--if you always get the right answer, you are not learning. The goal is to train yourself to think critically and understand the process, and to approach a problem on your own using recall of course content as a supplement. The value of an educator is to not simply lecture and provide answers witin the lectures; it is not the content they provide either; it is their ability to prepare students to engage in problems that will challenge them, and stimulate their fight-or-flight response in approaching a problem. It is their ability to walk with them and acclimate them to being self-active in their education and seek divergent solutions to novel problems.
